Goals

Goals are what will allow us to evaluate quality traffic from poor performing traffic.

Goals are a fundamental concept of any analytics package, and without goals, you have nothing more than a fancy hit counter. In other words, in order for us to say that site A is sending you much more valuable traffic than site B, we need some criteria to base that on, and that's where goals come in.

There's high performing traffic, low performing traffic, and everything in between. In order to analyze which sources and types of traffic are valuable, we need to track what those visitors are doing when they're on the site, what content works, how they're using the site, and ultimately figure out how to segment them and understand why they're doing what they're doing. Finally, we're going to measure how many folks and which segments of those people reach that final step and convert. Whether it's putting money in your bank account, or filling that lead-gen form, we get to tell our analytics package exactly what we consider a successful visit by setting a goal.

One very important point about goals is not to overlook intermediate goals, sometimes known as micro conversions. We're often so focused on that last step, such as a shopping cart checkout, that we forget about all the factors in between that contribute to the sale and get them to take that next step

How does Google Analytics work

Now the way Google Analytics works is based on the current generation of web analytics tools and has a very important difference from the older log file based systems in that it cuts out the web server logs, almost entirely from that process. When the web server returns the page back to your browser, it has some embedded JavaScript which is actually the code that runs Google Analytics. That Google Analytics code snippet collects information from your browser, and sends it directly to Google's powerful data centers. Now in addition to that JavaScript that runs in that standard web browser, we can also have that data sent back by mobile apps, and now even non-computer devices like coffee makers and virtually anything that can put the data in the right format.

The data is sent back to Google by what's known as the Measurement Framework. All that data is collected from those devices and visitors and ultimately stored in large databases. When you and I go log into Google Analytics, we open up the reporting database that's connected to those databases. When you think about it, your browser sends all the data to Google. And it actually has almost nothing to do with your web server that hosts your site, other than the fact that your site has that JavaScript snippet included as part of the page that it serves up. Depending on how much traffic you have, and the type of Google Analytics account you have, there can be a wide variance in how long it takes from the time a visitor sees the web page, until the data from that visit shows up in our reports.

Audience > Behavior Reports

Thinking of your website users as "dates" is a good analogy. If you treat returning visitors every time as if it was their first time, or if you treat first-time visitors as if they've known you for years, things are likely not going to work very well. So it makes sense to analyze Analytics data in segments of "first visit" vs "returning visitor" and compare how the site is performing for both groups.

Google Analytics track first visit vs. returning visitors through cookies. Which means that if the same person uses a different browser, they will be tracked as a new visitor. If you delete your cookies in the browser, you will also be tracked as a new visitor. Same thing for incognito browsing or private browsing windows. Same as well for visitors who visit from work and then from home.

You can find this new vs. returning information in the report under Audience > Behaviour > New vs. returning. For this report, the graph only shows you the total for both new vs. returning so here it makes sense to go in the data table, select both new and returning and click the button "Plot Rows" to see totals, new AND returning in the graph. It also makes sense to turn on the pie chart.

New Vs. Returning is not 100% accurate, and looking at returning visitors as one big group isn't entirely accurate either. To further segment the returning visitors, you can look at the report Audience > Behaviour > Frequency and Recency report, which gives us an idea of how many times users are coming back to the site. If you see big numbers at the bottom of the charts where it counts people that have done 100+ visits, this may be because of the employees of the organization that visit the site on a daily basis. You can also look at the section "Days since last session" in the same report, but keep in mind that over 30 days, it is more likely that users have gotten rid of their cookies, changed computers, etc.

The next report in this section is the Engagement report. And the session duration metric is a highly illuminating report, because it exposes just how misleading these averages can be, and it's pretty well known within user experience circles that most people spend less than ten seconds on a given page, which is backed up by this data. For example, in the Overview report, you may have seen that the average session is 1 minute 23 seconds whereas the vast majority of users only spend 10 seconds on the site. Don't rely too much on averages! And that means that this Engagement report can be very revealing of the true situation of a website.

A new visit starts any time you either open a new web browser, or let 30 minutes lapse, since the last page you saw on that site. So if you go have lunch, come back to your browser to the same website you were on earlier, that will be counted as a second visit to the site.

Intelligence report

Now, there are two different ways I use this tool. The first I'll call Forensic and the second we'll call Insights.

In Forensic mode what I'm really looking for, are things I see in various reports that are jumping out at me, but I can't necessarily explain it. Having a look at Daily Events where Google Analytics store particular events can help. Basically Google is going through every single report every day and logging the anomalies.

In Insight mode, I'm expecting Google to surface for me things that I would otherwise miss. In other words, one of the great things about these intelligence reports, is that it lets us look for that needle in a haystack, except I don't even necessarily know the needle exists, Google is going to figure that out too. Misunderstanding with segments

1. AND condition

Filter on session:
Page = Training page
And
Page = Homepage

This condition will work (surprisingly). Google here will show you sessions where people visited both these pages.

However:
Filter on user:
Page = Training page
And
Page = Homepage

This will NOT work.

But you can do this
Filter on user:
Page = Training page
(and then don't have either AND or OR, just create a new filter)
Page = Home page

This will bring you back all the users that have visited both the training page and the home page at some point (doesn't have to be in the same session).

2. Include vs. Exclude

Condition A: Include Page != ContactTrue (the thank you page after submitting a form)
Condition B: Exclude Page = ContactTrue

Corey: Home page > Blog > Training page > Video > About us
David: Home page > About us > Form Start > Form Submit > ContactTrue > Home page
    Condition A Condition B
Corey Y Y
David Y N

Condition A basically says "Show me all the users who have ever hit a page that was anything other than the ContactTrue page"
Condition B says: "Find the existence of people who visited this page and then exclude those people"

3. Filtering from both user and session

On session:
Country = United States
On user:
Revenue per user >= 100

- First, the USER will be filtered
- THEN the session-based condition will be applied

Therefore, let's say Alice has two sessions:

Session 1: $25, Canada
Session 2: $80, US

Alice passes filter 1 because she has $105 total revenue, but only her session 2 will appear in the report: Total revenue: $80, even though our filter indicated $100. That's because she did have $100 when we were including all the sessions that were later removed due to the session filter.

Another example
Filter on user:
Session duration > 300s

Alice
Session 1: 350s
Session 2: 200s

Bob
Session 1: 400s

Charlie
Session 1: 200s
Session 2: 200s

Alice will make it in (both sessions), Bob will make it in, Charlie is not included

Another example at the "hit" level
Filter on user:
Product Revenue per hit >= 50

Alice
Session 1: $25 $35 Total: $60
Session 2: $10 Total: $10

Bob
Session 1: $10 $75 Total: $85

Charlie:
Session 1: $55 $10 Total: $65
Session 2: $20 Total $20

Alice is out, Bob is in, Charlie is in and the total revenue of the segment is: $85 + $65 + $20 ($170)

4. Sequence segments

Filter include user
Step 1: Session duration >= 300s
Step 2: Transaction per hit > 0

Alice:
Session 1: No transaction, 300s
Session 2: 1 transaction, 200s

Bob: No transaction, 400s

Charlie:
Session 1: 1 transaction, 200s
Session 2: no transaction, 350s

Alice is in but Charlie is out because the steps are not in the proper order.

Custom reports

You have to be very careful with the granularity. For example, you can't build a report that tries to match page-level scope and session-level scope.

For example, whether you convert or not in a session is something that applies to that entire session. But how may page views a particular page has is a page level scope. So it makes no sense to create a content report that reports the number of page views and the conversion rate for a page. A page itself can't convert, a user converts in a session based on the goals you set up which may or may not include that page.

Documentation on the various scopes here: https://developers.google.com/analytics/devguides/reporting/core/dimsmets (select web mode)

A quicker way of building a custom report is to start from an existing report, go to the top of the page and click "customise". For example you could customize the language report to drill down into Country (instead of drilling down further into Language).

Cohort

A cohort is a group of people who are tied together by some common characteristic. They're generally grouped around a certain defined time period. It's not just looking at time periods such as people who buy on their first visit, or if they bounce. We're actually identifying a group of people and then we're following them as time passes. But we're not updating that definition, we stick with that original group

1. Create a new segment using Date of First visit as your criteria (to identify people who have seen that clever Superbowl ad you just ran)
2. Create the exact same segment but for one week prior

Date range cohort is limited to 31 days. And we also want to remember that these are user segments that we are using here, and so we know those can only go 90 days back
